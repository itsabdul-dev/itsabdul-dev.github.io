## 📝 Reflections

---

### 📄 Use of Markdown (.md)

Working with Markdown for this portfolio was a simple but genuinely useful experience.

I appreciated how a plain `.md` file could produce a clean, structured CV without needing any CSS, frameworks, or design tools. It made me think more carefully about content hierarchy — how to use headings, tables, and bullet points to guide the reader naturally through my information.

One thing that stood out was hitting Markdown's limitations. It doesn't natively support embedded video, so when I wanted to include my mock interview, I had to step outside of Markdown and write raw HTML directly in the file. That was a small but important lesson — knowing when a tool isn't enough, and being comfortable reaching for something else, is just as valuable as knowing the tool itself.

---

### 🎤 Mock Interview

The mock interview was one of the more uncomfortable parts of this assignment — and probably the most useful because of it.

Recording myself forced me to think about how I come across to someone who knows nothing about me. Watching it back, I noticed things I wouldn't have caught otherwise — my pacing, how I structure answers under pressure, and whether I'm actually communicating my skills clearly or just assuming the listener already understands my background.

It gave me a more honest picture of where I am right now as a communicator, and what I need to work on before I'm sitting in a real interview. That kind of feedback is hard to get any other way.

---

### 🚀 Deployment on GitHub Pages

Deploying to GitHub Pages made the whole project feel real in a way that just writing a `.md` file didn't.

Having a live public URL — something I can actually send to a potential employer or client — changed how seriously I took the content. Every push to the main branch updates the live site instantly, which made me more deliberate about what I committed and how the page looked at every stage.

It was also a low-pressure introduction to static site deployment. I could experiment, break things, and fix them without any real consequences. That kind of hands-on experience with version control and deployment workflows is something I'll carry into every project going forward.
